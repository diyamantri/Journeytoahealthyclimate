JOURNEY TO A HEALTHY CLIMATE
Heatwaves
Humans are producing higher and higher amounts of carbon dioxide and other potent heat-trapping gases. As these gases accumulate in the atmosphere, they essentially wrap an artificial blanket around the planet, trapping more and more of the Earth’s heat which would otherwise escape to space as a result the planet’s average temperature is increasing. Climate change made heatwaves at least 150 times more likely. This has resulted in an increase in drought conditions and crop failures. Heatwaves pose significant risk to the vulnerable population along with health, agriculture, and ecosystems. Unchecked, human-caused climate change could lead to the extinction of a third of the plant and animal species on the planet by 2050.
Methane and Other Gases
Methane is around eighty-three times more potent than CO2 over a twenty-year timeframe and thirty times more potent over a hundred-year timeframe. About a third of the Earth’s warming is attributed to methane, while the remainder is from a combination of nitrous oxide, halocarbons, chlorofluorocarbons, hydrochlorofluorocarbons, and other industrial chemicals. The production and use of fossil fuels, waste from agriculture, industrial activities, and appliances are the main sources of greenhouse gases other than carbon dioxide.
Air Pollution and Aerosols
Aerosols can directly affect climate by scattering sunlight and influencing cloud formation, leading to changes in temperature and precipitation patterns. They have both cooling and warming effects on the environment. Additionally, they can form atmospheric brown clouds, impacting regional climates and exacerbating extreme weather events. They lead to air pollution, which further compounds the issue, affecting human populations and straining healthcare systems.
Clouds
Clouds regulate the climate by reflecting sunlight away into the space and by trapping heat radiating from the Earth’s surface. Globally and considering all cloud types, on average, the cooling parasol effect is nearly twice as large as the insulating blanket effect. Removing all clouds would have a five times greater climate impact than increasing the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. Climate change affects cloud formation, which has an impact on energy balance.
Arctic Warming and the Jet Stream
As bright, white surfaces like sea ice and snow shrink, less of the sun’s energy is reflected back into space. Instead, it is absorbed into the climate system, which causes more ice and snow to melt. Since the mid-1990s, the Arctic has warmed at least three times faster than the rest of the world due to this vicious cycle, which is known as the ice-albedo feedback. Temperature variations, like the cold Arctic air pressing up against the warmer southern air, gives rise to jet streams. These create and steer most of the weather systems in temperate latitudes.
